Tokenization

How a computer splits text into words — and why that turns out to be a hard problem.

What tokenization is

Before a computer can do anything with text, it has to break the text into pieces. Those pieces are called tokens. This step is called tokenization.

It sounds easy. Split on spaces, and you are done:

"the cat sat"   →   ["the", "cat", "sat"]

That works for this sentence. It stops working almost immediately.

Why splitting on spaces fails

Look at this sentence:

"Dr. O'Neill isn't in — he's at St. Mary's, re: the U.S.A. deal."

Split it on spaces and you get problems straight away:

  • Mary's, keeps the comma stuck to it. That comma is not part of the name.
  • isn't stays as one piece. But it is really two words: is and not.
  • Dr. and U.S.A. contain full stops. If you split on full stops to fix the first problem, you break these.

Every rule you add to fix one case breaks another. This is real, and it is worth seeing. But it is not the interesting problem. The interesting problem is underneath.

The real problem: the word list is fixed, but language is not

First — what is a "model"?

This word is about to appear a lot, so let us pin it down. You do not need the details yet; you need enough to follow the next page.

In sessions 1 and 2 you wrote rules by hand. You decided a date looks like \d{2}/\d{2}/\d{4}, and you typed that out. The computer did what you said.

A model is the opposite. You do not write the rules. You show it a pile of examples — this is called training — and it works out the patterns itself. Afterwards you give it something new and it makes a guess.If you want a fuller answer now, the gentlest one on the internet is Google's "What is Machine Learning?" — about 20 minutes, no mathematics. Their ML Glossary is a good place to look up any term that appears before we teach it. Neither is required reading. We build all of this properly from session 20 onward.

To do that, a model has to keep some numbers for each word it knows — a handful of numbers per word, which it adjusts while it learns. That is all "a model" needs to mean today: a program that learned from examples, and keeps some numbers for every word it knows.

You will build your first one in week 6. For now, just hold on to the numbers-per-word part — the rest of this lesson turns on it.

Every model in this course needs a vocabulary. That is a fixed list of every token the model knows. The model builds this list once, from its training text, and then it is fixed forever.

It has to be fixed, because the model keeps a row of numbers for every token in that list — that is the "numbers per word" from the box above. Ten thousand words means ten thousand rows. So the size of the list decides the size of the model.

Here is the problem. Language does not have a fixed word list. People invent new words all the time — doomscrolling, rizz, ChatGPT-ing. No matter how much text you train on, you will meet new words later.

So the model breaks:

training:  vocabulary = every word seen in the training text
later:     model sees "doomscrolling"  →  not in the list  →  <UNK>

<UNK> means "unknown word". Every word the model has not seen becomes this same token. So the model cannot tell doomscrolling apart from any other unknown word. They all look identical to it.

That is bad enough. But look closer at doomscrolling. It contains scroll, a word the model knows well. The meaning is sitting right there in the letters. Splitting on spaces throws that away.

Think about this before reading on

You have a fixed word list. Language keeps inventing new words. You are not allowed to use <UNK>. What can you change?

Make the pieces smaller than words.

If your list holds word parts, you can spell any new word out of parts you already have. Take this to the extreme and a list of single letters can spell every word that exists.

But single letters are useless on their own. The letter s means nothing. And sentences become very long lists of letters.

So you have a trade-off: big pieces carry meaning but miss new words. Small pieces cover everything but carry little meaning. The answer is to sit in the middle. That is what the next section does.

Byte-Pair Encoding: let the data decide the pieces

We want word parts. But who decides where to split? A linguist could mark every prefix and suffix in English by hand. That would take years, and it would not work for other languages.

Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) has a better idea. It learns the pieces by counting. The rule is simple:

  1. Start with a list of single characters.
  2. Count every pair of neighbouring symbols in your text.
  3. Take the pair that appears most often. Join it into one new symbol. Add that symbol to the list.
  4. Repeat k times.

That is the whole algorithm. k is a number you choose. It decides how big your final list is.

Working through an example

Here is a small set of words. The number on the left is how often each word appears. The _ marks the end of a word.

5  l o w _
2  l o w e s t _
6  n e w e r _
3  w i d e r _
2  n e w _

list:  _, d, e, i, l, n, o, r, s, t, w

Now count the pairs. The pair e r appears in newer (6 times) and in wider (3 times). That is 9 — more than any other pair. So we join it:

merge 1:  e + r  →  er

5  l o w _
2  l o w e s t _
6  n e w er _
3  w i d er _
2  n e w _

Count again. Now er _ appears 9 times. Join it. Then n e appears 8 times (6 + 2). Join it. And so on:

merge 2:  er + _  →  er_
merge 3:  n + e   →  ne
merge 4:  ne + w  →  new
merge 5:  l + o   →  lo
merge 6:  lo + w  →  low

5  low _
2  low e s t _
6  new er_
3  w i d er_
2  new _
Look at what it learned

The algorithm created the symbol er_. In English, -er on the end of a word means "more of something" — wide becomes wider. It is a real piece of English grammar.

Nobody told the algorithm any of this. It does not know what English is. It only counted pairs. The grammar fell out of the counting.

It also learned that low and new are common enough to stay whole. Common words stay in one piece. Rare words get split up.

Why this fixes the problem

Take the word lower. It is not in our training list above. The old approach would call it <UNK> and give up.

BPE splits it into low + er_. Both pieces are already in the list. The model knows low, and it knows what er_ does to a word. So it can handle lower even though it has never seen it.

No unknown words. That is the point of the whole method.

Question 1 of 2

What happens if you increase k, the number of merges?

Correct answer: bigger list, fewer tokens.

Each merge adds one new symbol to the list. So more merges means a bigger list.

And each merge replaces two symbols with one. So the same text needs fewer tokens to write down.

This is the trade-off. A big list means short text, but the model gets big and slow. A small list means a small model, but the text becomes long. You cannot have both. Real systems usually pick somewhere around 30,000 to 50,000 merges.

Question 2 of 2

Why does BPE mark the end of each word with _?

Correct answer: so merges cannot run past the end of one word.

Without the marker, BPE would count the last letter of one word and the first letter of the next as a pair, just like any other. If that pair was common, it would join them. You would get junk symbols that span two words.

It has a second use too. Because er_ and er are different symbols, you can always rebuild the original text exactly, spaces and all. That matters for any model that has to write text, which you will meet in Unit III.

When you use BERT later, you will see words come out looking like this:

"doomscrolling"  →  ["doom", "##scroll", "##ing"]

The ## means "this joins to the piece before it". It does the same job as the _ in our example, just written differently. You already understand it.

The one thing to remember

Tokenization is not about splitting strings. It is about choosing the pieces your model is allowed to think in. Choose whole words, and new words become unknown. Choose word parts, and the model can handle anything.
