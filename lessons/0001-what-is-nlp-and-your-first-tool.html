What NLP is, and your first tool

Why language is hard for computers — and how to pull facts out of text with a pattern.

You already use this every day

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is getting computers to work with human language. You have used it several times today without noticing:

  • Your phone predicted the next word as you typed.
  • Your email put a message in the spam folder.
  • A search engine found a page that did not contain your exact words.
  • Something translated a sentence for you.
  • You asked a chatbot a question and got a sensible answer back.

Every one of these is in this course. By the end you will have built simplified versions of most of them.

So why is it hard?

Computers are good at exact things. Numbers, dates, IDs. Language is not exact. It is full of sentences that have more than one correct reading, and you resolve them without noticing.

Read this sentence:

The old man the boat.

Most people read it, stop, and read it again. It looks broken. It is not. Here man is a verb — it means "to staff". The sentence says: the old people are staffing the boat.

Your brain assumed "old man" was a noun phrase, then hit "the boat" and had to reverse. A computer has the same problem, and no instinct to fall back on.

Another:

I saw a man on a hill with a telescope.

Who has the telescope? You? The man? Is the telescope on the hill? All three readings are valid English. Nothing in the words tells you which is meant.

The point

Language is ambiguous: the same words can mean different things. You resolve ambiguity using knowledge of the world, and you do it so fast you never notice.

A computer has no world knowledge. Everything it knows must come from the text itself, or from what we build into it. Almost every technique in this course is a way of dealing with ambiguity.

We start at the bottom. Before anything clever, you need to get facts out of raw text.

Your first tool: regular expressions

A regular expression (regex) is a small language for describing patterns in text. You write a pattern; the computer finds every piece of text that matches it.

It is the oldest tool in NLP and still one of the most used. When you need to pull dates, phone numbers, or IDs out of documents, a regex is often the whole answer — no machine learning needed.Jurafsky & Martin, SLP3 chapter 2, §2.6. Note the section number — that chapter covers several topics and regex is not the first one.

In this course we use regex, not re

Python ships with a regex library called re. We will not use it. Install this instead:

pip install regex

Then import regex and use it exactly as you would re — same functions, same patterns. You lose nothing.

Why: re does not handle Indian scripts correctly. You will see this for yourself later in this lesson.

A real example

Say you have this line of text and you want the facts out of it:

"Call +91 98765 43210 before 15/03/2026 or email admin@manipal.edu"

Three patterns, three facts:

date   \d{2}/\d{2}/\d{4}        →  15/03/2026
phone  \+91 \d{5} \d{5}         →  +91 98765 43210
email  [\w.]+@[\w.]+            →  admin@manipal.edu

Read the date pattern out loud: "two digits, a slash, two digits, a slash, four digits." That is all a regex is — a description of shape.

Notice \+ in the phone pattern. + normally means "one or more", so to match a literal plus sign you put a backslash in front. This is called escaping, and forgetting it is the most common regex bug there is.

Why not re? Try it on Kannada

Here is the reason for that install. Take one word — ಕನ್ನಡ, the word "Kannada" — and ask each library to find the words in it.

text = "ಕನ್ನಡ ಭಾಷೆ"        # "Kannada language" — two words

re.findall(r"\w+", text)      # ['ಕನ', 'ನಡ', 'ಭ', 'ಷ']     ← four!
regex.findall(r"\w+", text)   # ['ಕನ್ನಡ', 'ಭಾಷೆ']            ← two

re found four words where there are two. It broke both words in half.

Why? Look inside ಕನ್ನಡ. It is five characters, and one of them is special:

ಕ   U+0C95   KANNADA LETTER KA        a letter
ನ   U+0CA8   KANNADA LETTER NA        a letter
್   U+0CCD   KANNADA SIGN VIRAMA      a combining mark  ←
ನ   U+0CA8   KANNADA LETTER NA        a letter
ಡ   U+0CA1   KANNADA LETTER DDA       a letter

The virama joins two consonants together. It is a mark, not a letter. And re's definition of \w — "a word character" — accepts letters and digits but rejects combining marks. So when re reaches the virama it decides the word has ended.

Every Indian script uses combining marks. So does Arabic, Hebrew, Thai. re's idea of "a word" was built around English, and it quietly falls apart everywhere else.

Why this matters more than it looks

This is not a cosmetic bug. In Unit II you will count words to build Bag of Words, TF-IDF and n-grams. If your word counts are doubled before you start, every number after that is wrong — and nothing will crash to tell you.

The failure is silent. That is the worst kind.

Try this before reading on

Write a pattern that matches a PIN code: exactly six digits.

Then a harder one: a roll number like MSIS2026001 — four letters, then seven digits.

PIN code: \d{6}

Roll number: [A-Z]{4}\d{7}

If you wrote \w{4}\d{7} for the second one — close, but not tight enough. \w matches letters and digits and underscore, so that pattern also accepts 12345678901 and msis2026001. Neither is a roll number.

Being exact about what you allow is most of the skill. A pattern that is too loose quietly lets rubbish through, and you will not notice until the data is wrong.

Question 1 of 2

What does [^aeiou] match?

Correct answer: any single character that is not a vowel.

The trap is ^. It has two different meanings depending on where it sits:

Inside square brackets, at the start: it means not. So [^aeiou] is "anything except a vowel".

Outside brackets: it means start of line. So ^The matches "The" only at the beginning.

Same symbol, two jobs. Expect to get this wrong once, then never again.

Question 2 of 2

Which pattern matches both colour and color?

Correct answer: colou?r.

The ? applies to the character just before it — u — and means "zero or one of these". So the u becomes optional. That is exactly the difference between the two spellings.

Why the others fail: colo*r makes the o optional, so it matches colr and colooor but never colour. colo+r has the same problem. colo.r needs exactly one character there, so it matches colour and coloXr, but not color.

The lesson: a quantifier attaches to the one thing immediately before it.

What to remember

The one thing

Language is ambiguous, and computers have no instinct for it. Everything in this course is a way of handling that.

But not every problem needs a clever model. When the thing you want has a shape — a date, a phone number, an ID — a regex will find it, and no amount of machine learning will beat it.
