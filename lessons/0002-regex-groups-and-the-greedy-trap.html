Capturing, choosing, and the greedy trap

Regular expressions, part two — pulling the pieces out, and the one bug everybody writes at least once.

Where we got to

Last session you learned to describe the shape of text: \d{2} for two digits, [Cc]at for either spelling, u? for optional.

That finds things. But finding is only half the job. Usually you want to pull the pieces out, or handle text that comes in more than one format. That is today.

Groups: getting the pieces out

Put round brackets around part of a pattern and it becomes a group. The regex engine remembers what matched inside each one.

text     "Exam on 15/03/2026."
pattern  (\d{2})/(\d{2})/(\d{4})

whole match  ->  15/03/2026
group 1      ->  15      the day
group 2      ->  03      the month
group 3      ->  2026    the year

The groups are numbered left to right by their opening bracket. Group 0 is always the whole match.

In Python:

import regex

m = regex.search(r"(\d{2})/(\d{2})/(\d{4})", "Exam on 15/03/2026.")
m.group(0)   # '15/03/2026'
m.group(1)   # '15'
m.group(3)   # '2026'

That is the difference between "there is a date here" and "the exam is in March".

A trap you will hit in the lab

Groups quietly change what findall gives back.

text = "Due 15/03/2026 and 01/04/2026"

regex.findall(r"(\d{2})/(\d{2})/(\d{4})", text)
# [('15', '03', '2026'), ('01', '04', '2026')]   <- tuples!

regex.findall(r"\d{2}/\d{2}/\d{4}", text)
# ['15/03/2026', '01/04/2026']                   <- the dates

With groups, findall returns the groups, not the whole match. If you want brackets for grouping but not for capturing, use (?:...) — a non-capturing group:

regex.findall(r"(?:\d{2})/(?:\d{2})/\d{4}", text)
# ['15/03/2026', '01/04/2026']

Alternation: this or that

The | symbol means "or".

Real data is rarely in one format. Some people write 15/03/2026, others write 15-03-2026. One pattern can take both:

\d{2}[/-]\d{2}[/-]\d{4}

matches  15/03/2026
matches  15-03-2026

Here a character class [/-] does the job, because it is a choice between single characters. For longer choices you need | with brackets:

(?:cat|dog)s?

on "a cat, two dogs"  ->  ['cat', 'dogs']

The brackets matter. cat|dogs? means "cat, or dogs?" — not what you wanted. | splits the whole pattern unless brackets stop it.

Word boundaries: the quiet bug

Search for cat in this word:

concatenate

It matches. Your pattern found "cat" inside "concatenate", and it was right to — you asked for those three letters, and there they are.

\b means word boundary: the edge between a word character and a non-word character. It matches a position, not a character.

pattern      concatenate    the cat sat    cat.
cat              yes            yes         yes
\bcat\b          no             yes         yes

Note the last column. \bcat\b still matches cat. — the full stop is not a word character, so the boundary is there. That is usually what you want: you are after the word, punctuation and all.

The same test in Kannada

Session 1 showed you that re breaks Kannada words at the virama. Watch what that does to \b.

In English, \bcat\b correctly refuses to match inside concatenate — "cat" is not a whole word there. Good. Now try the same thing on ಕನ್ನಡ, asking whether the fragment ಕನ is a whole word:

re.search(r"\bಕನ\b", "ಕನ್ನಡ")      # matches!    ← wrong
regex.search(r"\bಕನ\b", "ಕನ್ನಡ")   # no match     ← right

re says yes: it believes ಕನ is a complete word sitting inside ಕನ್ನಡ. It is not. It is the first half of one word.

The reason follows from session 1. \b is defined as "the edge of a \w run". If \w is wrong about where words end, \b inherits the error:

number of \b positions inside ಕನ್ನಡ

re      4      two at the ends, two invented at the virama
regex   2      just the ends — correct

The rule is identical in both libraries. It works in English and fails in Kannada. That is what "not Unicode compliant" actually looks like in practice — not a crash, just a wrong answer delivered confidently.

Why this matters beyond regex

You have just met, in miniature, the question this whole unit is about: where does a word end?

\b is one crude answer — "at the edge of a run of word characters". Even with the right library it falls apart on don't, on U.S.A., and on languages that do not put spaces between words at all.

A good library gets you a better wrong answer, not a right one. Session 3 is about what to do instead.

The greedy trap

This is the one. Everyone writes this bug once.

You want to pull the tags out of this text:

<b>bold</b> and <i>italic</i>

A tag is an angle bracket, some stuff, another angle bracket. So: <.*>. Obvious. Run it:

regex.search(r"<.*>", text).group()

# <b>bold</b> and <i>italic</i>

It matched the entire line. One giant "tag".

Why? Because * and + are greedy. They take as much as they possibly can, and only give characters back if the rest of the pattern cannot otherwise match.

Walk through it. < matches the first angle bracket. Then .* runs to the end of the line, grabbing everything. Now the pattern still needs a >, but there is nothing left. So .* backs up, one character at a time, until a > can match — and the first > it finds going backwards is the last one on the line.

The fix is ? after the quantifier. That makes it lazy: take as little as possible.

regex.search(r"<.*?>", text).group()      # <b>

regex.findall(r"<.*?>", text)
# ['<b>', '</b>', '<i>', '</i>']
The better fix

Lazy matching works. But there is a sharper way to say it:

regex.findall(r"<[^>]*>", text)
# ['<b>', '</b>', '<i>', '</i>']

Read it: "an angle bracket, then anything that is not a closing bracket, then a closing bracket." It cannot overshoot, because it is not allowed to cross a > in the first place.

Being precise about what you allow beats fixing it afterwards. That is the same lesson as the roll number in session 1, and it will keep coming back.

Question 1 of 2

On the text "aaa", what does the pattern a+? match?

Correct answer: just one letter a.

Read a+? as two separate things. a+ means "one or more a". The ? after it means "be lazy about it".

So: it must take at least one, and being lazy, it takes exactly one and stops.

The confusing part is that ? has two jobs, just like ^ did last session. After a character, a? means "optional". After a quantifier, +? means "lazy". Which one it is depends on what comes before it.

Question 2 of 2

Why does regex.findall(r"(\d{2})/(\d{4})", text) return tuples?

Correct answer: findall returns the groups when groups exist.

With no groups in the pattern, findall gives you a list of whole matches. Add brackets and it assumes the groups are what you actually wanted, so it gives you those instead — as a tuple per match.

Two ways out. If you do not need the pieces, drop the brackets. If you need brackets only for grouping, make them non-capturing with (?:...).

Changing text, not just finding it

regex.sub replaces what it matches. Groups make it powerful: \1, \2 in the replacement mean "whatever group 1 and 2 matched".

Reformat every Indian-style date into ISO format in one line:

regex.sub(r"(\d{2})/(\d{2})/(\d{4})", r"\3-\2-\1", "Exam on 15/03/2026.")

# 'Exam on 2026-03-15.'

Day, month and year get captured, then written back in a different order. No parsing, no loops.

This is the kind of thing you will do constantly in Unit I — cleaning text into a consistent shape before anything else touches it.

What to remember

The one thing

Quantifiers are greedy by default. They grab as much as they can. When a pattern matches far more than you expected, this is almost always why.

Add ? to make it lazy — or better, write the pattern so it cannot overshoot in the first place.
