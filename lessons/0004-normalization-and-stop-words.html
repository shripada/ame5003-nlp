Cleaning up text

Normalization and stop words — deciding which differences in text do not matter, and erasing them. The catch is that "does not matter" depends on the job.

Where we are

In session 3 you split text into tokens. So you now have a list of pieces. But look at these tokens:

"US"   "us"   "U.S."   "Us"

To you, some of these are the same and some are not. To the computer they are four different strings — nothing links them. Before you count words or search text, you usually want to collapse the differences that do not matter into one form.

That collapsing is called normalization. Here is the definition to hold on to for the whole lesson:

What normalization is

Normalization deliberately destroys information you have judged irrelevant.

That is not a side effect. It is the whole point. You are throwing away detail on purpose, so that things you consider "the same" really do count as the same. The skill is deciding what is safe to throw away — and that decision depends entirely on the task.

Case folding

The most common step: make everything lowercase.

"AMAZING"   "Amazing"   "amazing"   →   "amazing"

Three strings become one. Now every spelling of the word counts together when you count or search. For most Unit I work — counting words, searching documents — this is exactly what you want.

But notice what you just lost:

  • US (the country) and us (the word) both become us. They are now the same token.
  • Apple (the company) and apple (the fruit) merge too.
  • Polish (from Poland) and polish (for shoes) merge.

Case was carrying real meaning, and lowercasing threw it away. For search that is usually a fair trade — a user typing apple probably wants the company too. For a task where the capital letter is the signal, it is a mistake. You have to decide.

Think about this before reading on

You are building a system to pull company names out of news articles. Your first step is to lowercase everything, out of habit. What did you just break?

You erased the main clue you had. In English, a capital letter in the middle of a sentence is a strong hint that a word is a name — Apple, Rao, Manipal. Lowercase everything and Apple becomes apple, and the signal is gone.

This is the lesson in one example: a cleaning step that helps one task (searching) hurts another (finding names). There is no "correct" normalization. There is only "correct for this job".

Unicode: when identical-looking text is not equal

Here is a trap that catches people who have never thought about it. Two strings can look exactly the same on screen and still not be equal, because Unicode sometimes stores the same character in more than one way.

a = "café"     # é stored as ONE character
b = "café"     # é stored as  e + a separate accent mark

a == b    →    False
len(a)    →    4
len(b)    →    5

They print identically. One stores é as a single character; the other stores a plain e followed by a floating accent mark that sits on top of it. If a user types one form and your stored text has the other, your lookup silently fails — no error, just a match that never happens.

The fix is Unicode normalization: force every string into one standard form before you compare. The common choice is called NFC.

normalize("NFC", a) == normalize("NFC", b)    →    True
This matters more here, not less

This is not a European-accent curiosity. Indian scripts are built from base letters plus combining marks, so the same Kannada or Devanagari word can easily arrive in two encodings that look the same and compare unequal.

It is the same family of problem as the regex-not-re issue: Indian text has structure that naive handling gets wrong. A simple rule of thumb covers both: use regex to split, and NFC to compare. Normalize text to NFC the moment it enters your system, and this entire class of bug disappears.

Punctuation, numbers, and the rest

The other normalization steps are smaller and more obvious, and all of them are the same kind of decision — keep it or drop it, depending on the task:

  • Punctuation. For counting words you usually strip it, so that cat, and cat match. For finding sentence boundaries, or reading emoticons in a review, you keep it.
  • Numbers. Sometimes you replace every number with a single marker like <NUM>, because the exact value does not matter. Sometimes — a price, a date, an amount — it is the whole point.
  • Accents and spelling. résumé → resume, colour → color. Helpful for search, harmful if the difference carries meaning.

There is no fixed recipe. Each of these is a small version of the same question: is this difference noise, or is it signal, for the job I am doing?

Stop words

Stop words are the very common, low-content words — the, is, of, a, and. They appear in almost every sentence, so on their own they say little about what a text is about. A long tradition in text search removes them, to let the content words stand out.

Ready-made lists exist. NLTK's English list has about 200 words. Removing them is a one-liner: keep only the tokens that are not on the list.

"this is one of the best phones I have ever bought"
        ↓  remove stop words
"best phones ever bought"

What remains — best, phones, bought — is the gist. For search and for finding the topic of a document (which is most of Unit I's information-retrieval work), that is a genuine win: less to store, and the words that matter stand out.

The obvious step that is often the mistake

Now look at what is actually on a standard stop-word list. NLTK's list includes these words:

not    no    nor    against    only    very

Every one of those changes the meaning of a sentence. And not — the single most important word for saying something is bad — is treated as noise and thrown away. Watch what that does to a product review:

"would not recommend"
        ↓  remove stop words
"would recommend"

Removing stop words turned the sentence into its exact opposite. A sentiment system fed the second line would read an angry review as a happy one. This is the same lesson as VADER failing on nightmare back in lab 1: the obvious, routine step is sometimes the one that destroys your meaning.

When to remove stop words, and when not to

Remove them when you want the topic or keywords, and function words are just noise — search and information retrieval. You will do exactly this in lab 4.

Keep them when meaning depends on those words — sentiment, question answering, anything where not matters. As the glossary puts it: "to be or not to be" is entirely stop words.

Modern systems, in fact, tend to remove fewer stop words than older ones did — because the models that read the text can now use those small words instead of tripping over them.

Question 1 of 2

Why does lowercasing a document count as destroying information?

Correct answer: case can carry meaning, and merging it cannot be undone.

Once US and us have both become us, nothing in the text tells you which was which. The distinction is gone for good. That is fine when the distinction was noise for your task, and a real loss when it was signal. Normalization is a one-way door — that is exactly why the choice matters.

Question 2 of 2

For which task is removing stop words most likely to be a mistake?

Correct answer: deciding whether a review is positive or negative.

Sentiment lives in exactly the words a stop-word list throws away — not, no, very. Strip them and "would not recommend" becomes "would recommend". The other three tasks care about content words, so removing the function words helps them.

The one thing to remember

The whole lesson in two sentences

Every cleaning step — lowercasing, stripping punctuation, removing stop words — throws information away on purpose. Whether that helps or hurts depends entirely on the job, so there is no single correct way to clean text: you assemble the steps to fit the task in front of you.
