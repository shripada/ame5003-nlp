Named entity recognition

Finding the names in text — people, places, companies — which is harder than tagging words, because a single name can span several of them.

Where POS tagging ran out

Session 6 labelled each word one at a time: this word is a noun, that one is a verb. That works because a word's grammatical job is a property of the single word. Names are not like that.

The Reserve Bank of India raised rates.

Reserve Bank of India is one thing — an organization — spread across four words. Tagging each word on its own misses the point: the name is the whole span, not the pieces. That is the new problem this session solves.

What named entity recognition is

Named entity recognition (NER) finds the spans of text that are proper names, and labels each with its type. Run it on a sentence and you get back the names, typed:

"The Reserve Bank of India raised rates in Mumbai on Monday."

  The Reserve Bank of India   →  ORG    (an organization)
  Mumbai                      →  GPE    (a place)
  Monday                      →  DATE

The common entity types are these:

TypeWhat it coversExample
PERSONpeople, real or fictionalRao
ORGcompanies, agencies, institutionsInfosys
GPEcountries, cities, statesMumbai
LOCplaces that are not GPEs — rivers, mountainsHimalayas
DATEdates and periods, exact or relativeMonday, last week
MONEYamounts, with the unit$2 billion
This is the tool lab 2 was missing

In lab 2, regex pulled dates and rupee amounts out of bank messages perfectly — because those have a fixed shape. But it could not reliably get the merchant name, because names have no fixed shape: SWIGGY, Reserve Bank of India and Rao share no pattern a regex can match.

NER is the method for exactly that job. Instead of a hand-written pattern, it learns from examples what a name looks like — the same shift from rules to learning you saw when VADER failed on nightmare in lab 1.

The span problem, and how BIO solves it

Here is the difficulty. A POS tagger gives one tag per word, which is convenient — you met a machine for that in session 6. But an entity can cover several words. How do you mark a multi-word span using a system that only tags one word at a time?

The trick is a clever tagging scheme called BIO. Every word gets one of three kinds of tag:

  • B- — this word begins an entity of this type
  • I- — this word is inside (continues) the same entity
  • O — this word is outside any entity

Now the same one-tag-per-word machine can mark a span. Here is the Reserve Bank sentence in BIO:

The       B-ORG      ← the name starts here
Reserve   I-ORG      ← still inside it
Bank      I-ORG
of        I-ORG
India     I-ORG      ← still inside it
raised    O          ← outside — the name has ended
rates     O
.         O

Reading the tags back, the run of one B-ORG followed by four I-ORG reconstructs the whole span The Reserve Bank of India. This is the key idea: BIO turns "find the spans" back into "tag each word", so a sequence labeller can do the job.

Think about this before reading on

Why do we need a separate B- tag? Why not just tag every word of a company name ORG and every word outside O?

Because two names of the same type can sit side by side, and you need to know where one ends and the next begins. Take "... met Google Microsoft ..." as a list of two companies. Tag every word just ORG and you get:

Google   ORG
Microsoft ORG

— which reads as one two-word company, "Google Microsoft". With BIO you get B-ORG, B-ORG: two separate names, because a second B- means "a new entity starts here". The B- tag exists precisely to put a boundary between touching entities of the same type.

Why NER is genuinely hard

Two reasons, beyond the span problem.

The same name has different types. Washington is a person, a city, a state, and a football team, depending on the sentence. Apple is a company or a fruit. Only the context decides — the same lesson as POS ambiguity, one level up.

And taggers fail on names they were not trained on. Watch a real one trip on an Indian sentence:

"Rao paid Infosys 5000 rupees last week."

  spaCy finds:
    Rao            →  PERSON     ✓
    Infosys 5000   →  PRODUCT    ✗   (it merged the company and the amount, and mislabelled it)
    last week      →  DATE       ✓
  and it misses "5000 rupees" as money entirely.

It got the common English-shaped name (Rao, last week) right and mangled the part that looks unlike its training data. This is not a reason to avoid NER — it is the honest state of it. A model trained mostly on Western news text is weakest on exactly the names an Indian application cares about, which is worth knowing before you trust its output.

Question 1 of 2

What makes NER harder than the POS tagging from last session?

Correct answer: an entity can span several words.

A POS tag is a property of a single word, so one-tag-per-word fits perfectly. A name like Reserve Bank of India is one entity across four words, so you need a scheme — BIO — to mark where the span starts and ends. Both tasks use context; the span is the extra difficulty.

Question 2 of 2

In the BIO scheme, what does I-ORG mean?

Correct answer: a word inside an organization name that began earlier.

I- means "inside" — this word continues an entity that a B- tag already started. So B-ORG then I-ORG I-ORG is a single three-word organization. The first word is B-ORG; the continuation words are I-ORG.

Where NER is used

  • Information extraction — the lab-2 job done properly: pull the people, companies and places out of documents, contracts, news.
  • Search — a query for "Apple" can be steered to the company or the fruit once you know which the user means.
  • Redaction — find and hide every PERSON in a document to protect privacy.
  • Question answering — many questions ("who", "where", "when") are answered by an entity, so finding them is step one. You meet this in Unit III.

The one thing to remember

The whole lesson in two sentences

Named entity recognition finds and types the name spans in text, and its central trick is BIO tagging, which turns "find the spans" into "tag each word" so a sequence labeller can do it. It is the learned answer to the job regex could not finish in lab 2 — and, like any learned model, it is weakest on the names least like its training data.

That closes Unit I's language-analysis run — tag the words, then find the names. Next session changes gear from analysing one text to searching a whole collection of them, which is where the second half of Unit I begins.
