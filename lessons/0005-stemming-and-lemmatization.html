Stemming and lemmatization

Cutting a word down to a root form — the fast crude way, and the slow correct way — and knowing which one your task needs.

Where we are

In session 4 you collapsed harmless differences — case, accents, punctuation. There is one more difference worth collapsing, and it is the biggest: the same word wears different endings.

study    studies    studying    studied

These are one idea in four coats. For counting and for search you usually want them to count as the same thing — someone searching studying should find a page that said studies. So you cut each word down to a shared root.

There are two ways to do this, and the whole lesson is about how they differ and when to reach for each.

Stemming: chop the endings by rule

Stemming is the crude, fast way. It chops recognizable endings off a word by a fixed set of rules. The classic tool is the Porter stemmer, a list of rules like "if a word ends in -ing, remove it" applied in order.

Run the study family through it and they all land on the same string:

study      →  studi
studies    →  studi
studying   →  studi
studied    →  studi

Notice studi is not a real word. That is not a bug. The stemmer has exactly one job — make related words land on the same string — and it does that. Nobody ever reads studi; it is just an internal key. For search, this is perfect: all four spellings now match each other.

Here is another family, all collapsing cleanly:

argue  argued  argues  arguing    →    all become  argu

Where stemming shows its crudeness

Because it only chops by rule and knows nothing about meaning, it makes mistakes. Watch:

organization   →  organ      (the meaning is gone entirely)
was            →  wa         (just gibberish)
mice           →  mice       (unchanged — see below)
better         →  better     (no idea it relates to "good")
  • organization → organ — the rule stripped too much, and changed the meaning. This is called over-stemming.
  • was → wa — a rule fired that should not have, leaving nonsense.
  • mice → mice — the stemmer can only remove endings. An irregular plural like mice has nothing to chop, so it cannot reach mouse.

Stemming is fast and needs no dictionary, and for search it is often good enough. But it makes non-words and it mangles irregular ones. When you need a root that is a real word, you need the other method.

Lemmatization: look up the real root

Lemmatization also reduces a word to a root — but a real dictionary root, called the lemma. Instead of chopping, it looks the word up, so it can handle the cases stemming cannot:

mice     →  mouse       (irregular plural — correct)
was      →  be          (irregular verb — correct)
better   →  good        (knows the words are related)
studies  →  study       (a real word, not "studi")

Every output is a word you could find in a dictionary. That is the difference from stemming in one line: a stem is just a shared key; a lemma is the real base word.

The catch: lemmatization needs the part of speech

Here is the subtle part, and it is the whole reason lemmatization is harder than it looks. The root of a word can depend on how the word is used. Take meeting:

"we had a meeting"       meeting is a NOUN  →  lemma is  meeting
"they are meeting now"   meeting is a VERB  →  lemma is  meet

Same spelling, two different roots — and the only thing that tells them apart is the part of speech: is meeting a noun or a verb here? A lemmatizer that is not told the part of speech has to guess, and it usually guesses "noun". When the guess is wrong, it fails:

lemmatize("was")            →  wa      (guessed noun — wrong, and no better than stemming)
lemmatize("was", verb)      →  be      (told it is a verb — correct)

lemmatize("better")         →  better  (guessed noun — wrong)
lemmatize("better", adj)    →  good    (told it is an adjective — correct)

So lemmatization is only as good as the part-of-speech information you feed it. Who provides that? You do not want to label every word by hand. A tool like spaCy works out the part of speech for you automatically and lemmatizes in one step — which is why it is the easy choice for real work. (Working out the part of speech is its own topic: session 6.)

Think about this before reading on

You are building a search engine over a few million documents. It has to be fast, and the roots are never shown to a user — they are only used internally to match a query against a document. Stem, or lemmatize?

Stem. Speed matters, nobody sees the roots, and non-words like studi are completely fine because they are only used as internal match keys. A dictionary lookup per word would be slower and buys you nothing here.

Flip the task — you are showing the root words back to a user, or feeding clean words into a model — and the answer flips to lemmatize, because now studi and wa would be an embarrassment. Same idea as lesson 4: the right choice depends on the job.

Question 1 of 2

The Porter stemmer turns organization into organ. Why is this not necessarily a problem?

Correct answer: for search, all that matters is that related words share a key.

A stem is only an internal match key — nobody reads it. If organization and organizations both become organ, a search for one finds the other, which is the goal. It does become a real problem if organ (the instrument or body part) also stems to organ and gets wrongly matched — that over-collapsing is the price of stemming's crudeness. For a task that needs meaning, use a lemma.

Question 2 of 2

Why can a lemmatizer turn was into be, when the Porter stemmer only manages wa?

Correct answer: the lemmatizer looks the word up, so it knows irregular forms.

Stemming only chops endings, so an irregular form like was → be or mice → mouse is out of its reach — there is no ending to remove. Lemmatization uses a dictionary of forms, so it can map any inflection to its base word. The cost is that it needs to know the part of speech, and it is slower.

The one thing to remember

The whole lesson in two sentences

Stemming chops endings by rule — fast, no dictionary, output need not be a real word — and it is the right tool for search. Lemmatization looks up the real dictionary root — correct, needs the part of speech, slower — and it is the right tool when you need real words.

That closes the text-cleaning arc: tokenize, normalize and remove stop words, and now cut to a root. Next session we start working with the cleaned words, beginning with part-of-speech tagging — the very thing lemmatization was asking for.
