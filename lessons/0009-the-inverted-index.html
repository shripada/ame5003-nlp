The inverted index

Keep only the ones. For each word, a list of the documents it appears in — and a fast way to answer a query by walking two lists at once.

Picking up where session 8 stopped

The incidence matrix was 99.8% zeros, and those zeros carried no information we cared about. The fix we arrived at was: stop storing the zeros; keep only the ones. This session turns that one sentence into the data structure that runs real search engines.

Building the inverted index

An inverted index has two parts:

  • A dictionary — every term in the collection.
  • For each term, a postings list — the document IDs where that term appears, kept in sorted order.

It is called inverted because it flips the natural direction. A document normally points to the words in it; here each word points to the documents it is in. Built from the same six Shakespeare plays as last session (numbered 1–6), it looks like this:

Antony     →  1 → 2 → 6
Brutus     →  1 → 2 → 4
Caesar     →  1 → 2 → 4 → 5 → 6
Calpurnia  →  2
Cleopatra  →  1
mercy      →  1 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6
worser     →  1 → 3 → 4 → 5

Compare this with the matrix row for Caesar, which was 1 1 0 1 1 1 — six cells to say the same thing the postings list 1 2 4 5 6 says with five short numbers, and no wasted zero. A word that appears in three documents needs a list of three, whether the collection has six documents or six billion. The size of a postings list depends on the word, not on the size of the collection. That is the whole saving.

Answering a query: merge two postings lists

Now the query. Take one AND from last session's example:

Brutus AND mercy

You need the documents that appear in both postings lists — their intersection. The clever part is how. Because both lists are sorted, you do not compare every pair. You walk both lists at the same time with two pointers, and at each step you advance the one pointing at the smaller document ID:

Brutus:  1 → 2 → 4
mercy:   1 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6

step 1:  1 vs 1   equal   → keep 1, advance both
step 2:  2 vs 3   2 < 3   → advance Brutus (2 can't be in mercy now)
step 3:  4 vs 3   4 > 3   → advance mercy
step 4:  4 vs 4   equal   → keep 4, advance both
         Brutus is finished → stop

result:  1 → 4      (Antony and Cleopatra, Hamlet)

Each pointer only ever moves forward, so you pass through each list once. If the lists have lengths x and y, the work is about x + y — you never do x × y comparisons. That linear cost is the reason search feels instant, and it is only possible because the lists are sorted. Keeping postings sorted at build time is what buys you the fast query.

Why sorting is the whole trick

When you see 2 in one list is smaller than 3 in the other, you know 2 cannot appear later in the second list — everything ahead is bigger. So you skip it and move on, with no going back. Unsorted lists give you no such guarantee, and you are forced back to checking every pair against every pair.

Finishing the real query

The full query was Brutus AND Caesar AND NOT Calpurnia. Same tool, applied in turn:

Brutus  1 2 4   INTERSECT   Caesar  1 2 4 5 6     →   1 2 4
        1 2 4   AND NOT     Calpurnia  2           →   1 4

NOT Calpurnia just means "drop any document that is in Calpurnia's list" — remove 2, leaving 1 4: Antony and Cleopatra and Hamlet. The same answer as the matrix gave in session 8, reached without storing a single zero.

One optimisation worth knowing

For Brutus AND Caesar AND Calpurnia, start with the shortest postings list. Calpurnia is in just one document, so intersecting it first leaves at most one candidate to check against the others. Process terms from rarest to commonest, and you do the least work. This is a standard query-optimisation, and a likely viva question.

Think about this before reading on

Using the index above, work the merge for Brutus AND Antony by hand. Brutus is 1 2 4; Antony is 1 2 6. Which documents come back?

Brutus:  1 → 2 → 4
Antony:  1 → 2 → 6

1 vs 1   equal   → keep 1, advance both
2 vs 2   equal   → keep 2, advance both
4 vs 6   4 < 6   → advance Brutus
         Brutus finished → stop

result:  1 → 2

Antony and Cleopatra and Julius Caesar. The 4 and the 6 never match, and the two-pointer walk skips straight past them without ever comparing 4 to 1 or 2.

Question 1 of 2

What does a postings list contain?

Correct answer: the sorted IDs of the documents that contain one term.

The dictionary holds the terms; each term points to its postings list — the documents it appears in, in sorted order. Caesar → 1 2 4 5 6. Sorted, because that is what makes the merge fast.

Question 2 of 2

Why can two postings lists be intersected in roughly x + y steps instead of x × y?

Correct answer: both lists are sorted, so each pointer only moves forward.

Sorted order means that when one ID is smaller than the other, it cannot appear later in the other list, so you skip it and never look back. Each list is traversed once — about x + y steps total. Lose the sorting and you are back to checking every pair.

The one thing to remember

The whole lesson in two sentences

An inverted index stores, for each term, a sorted list of the documents that contain it — only the ones, never the zeros. Queries are answered by merging these sorted lists with a two-pointer walk that runs in linear time, which is exactly why web-scale search returns in milliseconds.

But so far a document either matches or it does not — the result is an unordered set. Real search puts the best document first. Ranking is the next session, and it is where numbers replace the plain 0s and 1s.
