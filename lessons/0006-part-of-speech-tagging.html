Part-of-speech tagging

Labelling each word with its grammatical job — and why the same word needs a different label depending on the words around it.

Why this comes right after lemmatization

Last session hit a wall: to lemmatize meeting correctly, you had to know whether it was a noun ("a meeting") or a verb ("they are meeting"). We said "a tool works out the part of speech for you". This is that tool.

Part-of-speech tagging (POS tagging) labels every word in a sentence with its grammatical category — noun, verb, adjective, and so on:

They    book    flights   daily
PRON    VERB    NOUN      ADV

The word classes

Words fall into a small number of grammatical classes. You already know most of them from school; here are the ones that matter, with what each does:

ClassJobExamples
Nouna thing, person, place, ideaphone, Manipal, idea
Verban action or staterun, book, is
Adjectivedescribes a nounold, blurry, best
Adverbdescribes a verb or adjectivedaily, very, quickly
Pronounstands in for a nounI, they, it
Determinerintroduces a nounthe, a, this
Prepositionrelates a noun to the reston, in, to

There is a useful split here. Nouns, verbs, adjectives and adverbs are "open" classes — new ones get invented all the time (to google, selfie). Determiners, pronouns and prepositions are "closed" — the list barely changes in a lifetime. No one invents a new word for the. That is also, not by coincidence, the same set of little words that a stop-word list removes.

The real problem: the same word has no fixed class

If every word had one grammatical class, tagging would be a dictionary lookup. It is not, because a word's class depends on how it is used. Look at book:

"Give me the book."        book  →  NOUN   (a thing)
"I can book a flight."     book  →  VERB   (an action)
"They book flights daily." book  →  VERB   (an action)

Same four letters, different job each time. No dictionary can tell you which is which — only the surrounding words can. the book must be a noun (a determiner is followed by a noun); can book must be a verb (a word like can is followed by one). The tagger reads the whole sentence and uses each word's neighbours to decide.

This is why POS tagging is called a sequence-labelling task: you do not tag a word on its own, you tag it in the sequence it sits in. The context is not a nice-to-have; it is the only thing that resolves the ambiguity.

Think about this before reading on

Here is a sentence that is famously hard: "The old man the boat." It is a correct English sentence. What grammatical job is each word doing?

The old means "the old people" — old is standing in as a noun. man is the verb: to man a boat means to staff it. So it reads "the old people staff the boat".

You almost certainly first read man as a noun and hit a dead end — that is why it is called a "garden-path" sentence. And here is the honest part: a modern tagger gets this wrong too. spaCy tags man as a noun, exactly the mistake a human first makes. Taggers are about 97% accurate, but genuinely ambiguous sentences still beat them.

Tagsets: how fine-grained do you want to be?

How many labels are there? It depends which tagset you choose — and there are two common levels of detail.

The Universal POS tagset is coarse: about 17 tags, the broad classes above — NOUN, VERB, ADJ, ADV, DET, and so on. Easy to read, and the same across languages.

The Penn Treebank tagset is fine: about 45 tags. It splits each broad class into detailed ones — a noun can be NN (singular) or NNS (plural); a verb can be VB (base form), VBZ (3rd-person singular), VBP (other present), and more.

The tool gives you both at once. Here is one sentence with both tagsets:

WordUniversalPenn Treebank
TheyPRONPRP (personal pronoun)
bookVERBVBP (present, not 3rd-sing)
flightsNOUNNNS (plural noun)
dailyADVRB (adverb)

Use the coarse tagset when you only need "is this a noun or a verb" — for example, to feed the lemmatizer from session 5. Use the fine one when the detail matters, such as telling singular from plural. Neither is more correct; they are different zoom levels on the same thing.

Where POS tagging is used

  • Lemmatization — the session-5 payoff. The lemmatizer needs the part of speech, and this is where it comes from.
  • Named entity recognition — next session. Knowing the nouns is a strong first clue to where the names are.
  • Parsing and downstream tasks — most classical NLP pipelines tag first, because so much later work leans on knowing each word's job.
Question 1 of 2

Why can POS tagging not be done as a simple dictionary lookup?

Correct answer: a word's class depends on the words around it.

book is a noun in "the book" and a verb in "can book". A dictionary would have to list both and could not choose between them — only the context decides. That is the whole reason POS tagging is a sequence-labelling task and not a lookup.

Question 2 of 2

You need to know only whether each word is a noun, verb, or adjective, to feed a lemmatizer. Which tagset fits best?

Correct answer: the Universal tagset.

The lemmatizer only needs the broad class — noun vs verb vs adjective — so the coarse 17-tag set is exactly right. The fine Penn distinctions (singular vs plural noun, which present tense) are detail the lemmatizer does not use here. Match the tagset to what the task needs.

The one thing to remember

The whole lesson in two sentences

Part-of-speech tagging labels each word with its grammatical job, and it needs the whole sentence because the same word can be a noun in one place and a verb in another. That reliance on context — not a dictionary — is what makes it a real task, and it is the same reason the next step, finding names, is harder still.
