Ranking results with TF-IDF

Not just which documents match, but which match best — and why the score needs a logarithm in two different places.

Where Boolean search ran out

The inverted index answered "which documents match?" — and gave back an unordered set. A document either matched or it did not. That is fine for six plays. Type a query into a web search and it matches millions of pages. An unordered set of a million results is useless. You need the best ones first.

So we move from matching to ranking: give each matching document a score for the query, then sort by score. The classic scoring recipe, still the baseline everywhere, is TF-IDF. It is built from two ideas, and we build it the same way.

Idea 1 — term frequency: how often the word appears

A document that says car ten times is probably more about cars than one that says it once. So the first idea is simple: score a document by how many times the query term appears in it. This count is the term frequency (TF).

Why the raw count is wrong

Raw count over-rewards repetition. Is a document with car 100 times really 100 times more relevant than one with it once? No. It is more relevant — but nowhere near 100 times. Relevance grows with count, but it grows slower and slower. The tenth mention adds much less than the second.

The fix is a logarithm, which is exactly the function that grows fast at first and then flattens. We use a log-weighted term frequency:

weight = 1 + log₁₀(tf)      (when tf > 0)

Watch what it does to the count:

appears (tf)1101001000
weight1.02.03.04.0

Every tenfold increase in the count adds just 1 to the weight. Going from 1 mention to 10 matters as much as going from 10 to 100. That is where the first logarithm comes from: it turns "diminishing returns" into arithmetic.

Idea 2 — inverse document frequency: how rare the word is

Term frequency alone has a worse problem. Consider the query the best car. The word the appears many times in every document, so by term frequency it scores enormously — and it tells you nothing about which document is a good match. A common word is a bad discriminator; a rare word is a good one. If two documents share the word insurance, that is a far stronger signal than sharing the.

So we want to down-weight common words and up-weight rare ones. Measure how common a word is by its document frequency (df) — the number of documents that contain it. Then weight by the inverse, squashed with a log again:

idf = log₁₀(N / df)        N = total number of documents

Here are real values from a collection of N = 806,791 documents:

termin how many docs (df)idf
auto 6,723 2.08
car 18,165 1.65
insurance 19,241 1.62
best 25,235 1.50

The rarer the word, the higher its idf: auto (in fewer documents) outranks best (in more). And the important edge case:

Stop words down-weight themselves

A word in every document has df = N, so its idf is log₁₀(N/N) = log₁₀(1) = 0. Its weight is zero.

Remember the stop-word problem from session 4? IDF handles it for free. You do not need a hand-written list to kill the — it appears everywhere, so its idf is near zero, and it contributes almost nothing to any score. The maths does what the stop-word list was trying to do, without the list.

Putting them together

The TF-IDF weight of a term in a document multiplies the two ideas:

tf-idf  =  (1 + log₁₀ tf)  ×  log₁₀(N / df)
             how often it appears          how rare it is
             IN THIS document              ACROSS all documents

This is high in exactly the right case: a term that appears often in this document but is rare across the collection — in other words, a word that is distinctive to this document. It is low for a word that is rare here, and near zero for a word that is everywhere.

To score a whole document for a query, add up the tf-idf weights of the query terms, then sort the matching documents by that total. The document with the highest score comes first. That is ranked retrieval.

The two logarithms, side by side

Both logs are there to squash a big range into a manageable one, but for different reasons:

  • On TF: because relevance has diminishing returns — the 100th mention barely adds to the 10th.
  • On DF: because word rarity spans a huge range — from a word in ten documents to one in a million — and a raw ratio would let rare words dominate wildly.
Think about this before reading on

Two documents both match the query car insurance. Document A says insurance 9 times; document B says the 50 times and insurance once. By TF-IDF, which ranks higher — and why is that the right answer?

Document A. Its insurance weight is (1 + log 9) × 1.62 ≈ 1.95 × 1.62 ≈ 3.2. Document B's the contributes almost nothing — its idf is near zero, no matter that it appears 50 times — and its single insurance scores 1 × 1.62 = 1.62.

So A wins, which is correct: A is genuinely more about insurance, while B just happens to use a common word a lot. This is TF-IDF doing its whole job in one comparison — rewarding the distinctive word, ignoring the common one.

Question 1 of 2

Why is the term frequency put through a logarithm instead of used raw?

Correct answer: relevance grows with count, but less and less each time.

A word appearing 100 times does not make a document 100 times more relevant than one where it appears once. The log captures those diminishing returns: each tenfold rise in the count adds the same fixed amount (1) to the weight.

Question 2 of 2

A word appears in every single document in the collection. What is its idf?

Correct answer: zero, so it contributes nothing to any score.

If df = N then idf = log(N/N) = log(1) = 0. A word that is everywhere cannot distinguish between documents, and IDF gives it exactly zero weight — which is why TF-IDF suppresses stop words without needing a stop-word list.

The one thing to remember

The whole lesson in two sentences

TF-IDF scores a term high when it appears often in this document but rarely across the collection — frequent-here times rare-everywhere — so distinctive words drive the ranking and common words fall away. The two logarithms are there to tame diminishing returns on the count and to compress the huge range of word rarity into a usable weight.
